---
title: "An Anecdote"
date: 2025-08-16
---

If you can force your heart and nerve and sinew
To serve your turn long after they are gone,
And so hold on when there is nothing in you
Except the Will which says to them: ‘Hold on!’

When I first heard this, I understood the poetry. Kipling says we must rise to the occasion, never retreat from weakness, and emerge all the stronger from but a few wisps of strength. Sure, I resonated with the message. But life had thus far been a breeze.

When I first heard this, I did not see it, did not taste it, did not feel it. The words were just that, just words. You cannot truly foster the feeling of an emotion without the actual feeling; you cannot ever make it resonate. There were writers and poets of immensity who have tried—Kipling being one of them—but will never and can never, on account of psychology, succeed in truly placing their writing’s emotion in the hearts of their readers.

That feeling requires being felt, requires experience, requires loss, pain, remembrance, and sometimes death.

When I first heard this poem, although I understood it, I did not see it, did not taste it, did not feel it, until the weakness Kipling spoke of became my own. It was not until Sophomore year of High School that I felt it.

Let medical records say what they will. I will give my own recollection.

I woke up at six, showered, dressed, brushed my teeth, and left. At school, I did my homework until the bell rang. In First Period, I worked on a Python project when I was supposed to be paying attention. In Second, I had a stroke. And in Third, I was at Marin General, lying down, barely conscious.

Hard as it is to replicate biology, I can vaguely recall the feeling. To an already exhausted high school brain was added an illogical chaos, so that my thoughts became fuzzy, unordered. I had no fear, for I had not the wherewithal nor the wits for fear.

Submerged, barely-breathing, numb, and suffocating beneath the weight of the stroke, where a few chronophagic moments of painful relief turned into hot trepidation:—fear I had half-dreamed forsaken encroached frostily on my psyche; from that harrowing thought, that hot, bubbling emotion of breathlessness, of my blocked artery, time would see my mind returned: two weeks later, I awoke in UCSF. My legs and my arms were deemed “slightly impaired” even though to me they worked fine. I was told a six months period of initial rehab and painstakingly simple rest was to be my medicine. That, and a month’s worth of steroids. Okay, Doc. No problem.

Now, two months have passed. School starts tomorrow. The me of today and the me of then are two entirely different people, physically and mentally. Allow me a brief moment to share my progress, and perhaps you may then imagine the me of tomorrow.

My mind, freed from its hospitalized shackles, now finds within its revived entirety a new shining passion, a new shimmering dream. That dream is hope. That dream is biology, that is, biology for the sake of steel. Steel that does not bleed when struck, but only warps when blown; that does not oxidize with every cell division but lingers, extraterrestrial, supernatural, on the earth as Aurora Borealis lingers in the sky—ad infinitum.

What causes a stroke? What gave me this weakness? I want to know. My first biological undertaking, I have decided, will be to build an electroencephalography (EEG) machine for measuring brain waves.

But the day will come, in a future unsupervised by this umbral shade, when I will dream of Frankenstein’s monster in its Kafkaesque creation: and imbue tangibility.

For if death cannot kill me, I will manifest destiny.
