When 2026 began, I certainly could not have anticipated my hackathon victories. Nor could I have foreseen how well versed I would become in web development as a result. My tastes have changed also, and I have taken into my possession the most sought-after quality, that arises only thereafter the mind is battered and repaired under the strain of competition, that superior quality of just ideation. Just ideation is the ability to comprehend the goal, then to form an idea which correctly reaches it, and in doing so, benefits a certain part of society. It’s a core facet of hackathon participation; hence, my strengthening it.

Now, that said, my hackathon journey has also taught me that there are not nearly enough hackathons—and I live in the Bay Area, ground zero for tech and AI. So, with the help of the Computer Science Club and my Co-President, Stanley Ho—basically just him actually—we will create our school’s first hackathon, MarinHacks, to be held August 2nd of 2026.

Our master sheet with all of the sponsors, judges, and miscellaneous things listed has reached a threshold where I must now scroll and slowly read and re-read everything. But of the sponsors, none have pledged a single dollar. What they have pledged are credits or subscriptions. As much as I’m not complaining (since they did offer to contribute something), I have also since removed the “equivalent goods” part of the email, so that companies will hopefully offer money first. I promise I’m not greedy. It’s just that, without sufficient funds, our poor hackers will be forced to eat CostCo pizza and fast food slop. I am of the opinion that such treatment is medieval and adjacent to the gulag because, when I’m at a hackathon for twelve hours straight and get served that, I’m on the verge of crashing out. We should be getting healthy, nutritious, flavor-filled meals—like cheesy, cholesterol-filled Mexican food. Yep, that’s where it’s at alright. That’s flavortown, baby.

Yet, despite the hardship, and the emailing every lunch period, it’s actually been quite fun working on this hackathon. I’ve enjoyed it. Even if the other members of the club flake every time they’re asked to do something that isn’t Geoguessr or Minesweeper, working alongside my trustworthy Co-President is something I really enjoy.

And for all those Computer Science clubs out there, I highly recommend hosting a hackathon. Not only does it beautify your LinkedIn, but it actually gives you real-world event hosting skills and isn’t that much of a time commitment—if you don’t consider lunch periods that much of a time commitment, which apparently, some people are VERY stingy about using.

In any case, MarinHacks is set to be hosted on August 2nd, 2026. If all goes well, which I’m sure it will, this will be an event that recurs annually in the future.

(But man, feeding hundreds of people good food is so damn expensive.)
