We all know what radios are. We’ve all been in the car, listening to Maroon V and Bruno Mars on repeat, or Michael Jackson and The Police if we get lucky. We’ve all gone through a tunnel under a mountain, singing so electrically a random lyric, only for our voice to stutter and depressingly fade out as static drowns out our song.

But why?

Understanding that requires understanding the inner workings of radios.

A radio is a device that interprets radio waves. Radio waves are a type of electromagnetic radiation with the lowest frequencies and highest wavelengths, and they travel at the speed of light. Using resonance, our radios may be attuned to catch certain frequencies, a phenomenon we call “switch the channel, this song sucks.”

Because I was looking into wireless data transfer via Arduino, I naturally stumbled upon radios. I purchased a transmitter and receiver, so that I could experiment with them, and soon became enamored by how they worked.

These $3 parts were locked at one frequency, specifically 433 MHz, because their price was proportional to their frequency flexibility. Yet I gained much from writing code and testing their functionality. You may imagine, but cannot justly gather without experiencing it yourself, my excitement when the first results were yielded: I could now turn off and on an LED using a button within a second, separate circuit.

When in the grasp of a newfound technology, there is always that godlike emotion of endless possibilities; discovering that new path, you realize there are a million that follow and break off from it, all entering their respective domains. You may continue down the main path if it satisfies no further inquiry, or you may choose to take “the road less traveled by,” and surely if you enter upon it with sufficient willpower, you will come to discover a new domain, a new technology, and realize your deviation from conformity has “made all the difference” (Robert Frost, The Road Not Taken).

That is how I felt when my first experiment with radio technology bore fruit. From then, the complexity of my implementation left the realm of the simple wireless light switch. The next project was a grabber claw, which was capable of picking up small, lightweight things. I pursued more things from then on, but I feel naming them would only serve to prove a proven point, and therefore a description of them would be meaningless and verbose.

If you are beginning your journey with Arduino and are interested in wireless technology, consider purchasing a transmitter and receiver and experimenting with them. You may be ferried to new frontiers.
